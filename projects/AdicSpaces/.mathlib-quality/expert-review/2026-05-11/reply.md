## Mathematical idea

For **Lane B**, the correct statement is:

```
O(D₀) → ∏ᵢ O(Dᵢ)
```

is faithfully flat for a rational covering. This is a cover theorem, not a single rational-localisation theorem. A single rational restriction may fail to be injective or topologically inducing after completion because the target topology can be strictly finer. Faithful flatness of a single map would require extra hypotheses, such as a spectrum-surjectivity condition for that single map, and is not part of Wedhorn's acyclicity proof.

For **Lane A**, let

```
B = A⟨X⟩ / (f - X).
```

If `A⟨X⟩` is a noetherian Tate ring and `(f - X)` is closed, then `B` with the quotient topology is a noetherian Tate ring. A ring of definition is the image of a ring of definition of `A⟨X⟩`; an ideal of definition is the image of the principal ideal generated by the chosen pseudo-uniformizer. The topologically nilpotent unit descends. Completeness and Hausdorffness follow from quotienting by a closed ideal.

Once `B` is available as a Tate ring, polynomial density in `B⟨Z⟩` is just the usual truncation argument: a restricted series is the limit of its partial sums because its coefficients tend to zero. This is the right way to close the reverse round trip.

For **Lane C**, the per-`E` approach is legitimate. It says: instead of constructing one global refinement map and then fighting choice-induced transport, prove gluing locally over each target `E`, then stitch the local outputs using separation over `E`. This is exactly the kind of reindexing that Lean benefits from. The textbook proof may split at the `D₀` level, but the per-`E` formulation is equivalent provided the local coverings really refine `E` and the final stitching theorem is already proved.

The C1 theorem should be formulated intrinsically:

```
for each rational target E and each point v ∈ E,
there is a plus-piece neighbourhood of v contained in E;
finite extraction gives the required standard refinement.
```

That is closer to "rational plus-pieces form a basis after Corollary 7.32" than to any particular guessed expression like a product of ratios.
